Learning Guide: Magnetism, Geometry, and Oxidation State

This guide explains how to determine the properties of coordination complexes based on their magnetic behavior.

Phase 1: Magnetism and Unpaired Electrons

The magnetic properties of a coordination complex are determined by its electrons.

  • Paramagnetism: The substance is weakly attracted to a magnetic field. This occurs when the complex has one or more unpaired electrons.
  • Diamagnetism: The substance is weakly repelled by a magnetic field. This occurs when all electrons in the complex are paired.

The question gives us the magnetic behavior, which provides a huge clue about the electron configuration of the central metal ion.

Phase 2: Geometry and Hybridization

Valence Bond Theory explains the geometry of complexes by proposing that the metal ion's atomic orbitals hybridize (mix) to form a new set of degenerate orbitals that are ready to accept electron pairs from the ligands.

The type of hybridization depends on the coordination number and the ligands:

  • Coordination Number 4:
    • sp³ hybridization: Leads to a tetrahedral geometry. This usually happens with weak-field ligands.
    • dsp² hybridization: Leads to a square planar geometry. This usually happens with strong-field ligands that can force d-electrons to pair up, freeing an inner d-orbital.
  • Coordination Number 6:
    • sp³d² hybridization: Octahedral geometry.
    • d²sp³ hybridization: Octahedral geometry.

Phase 3: The Summit - The JEE Main Question

Original Question: From the magnetic behaviour of [NiCl₄]²⁻ (paramagnetic) and [Ni(CO)₄] (diamagnetic), choose the correct geometry and oxidation state.

Let's analyze each complex individually.

Analysis of [Ni(CO)₄]

  • Oxidation State: The carbonyl ligand (CO) is neutral. Therefore, the overall charge of the complex (0) is the oxidation state of the Nickel. Ni is in the 0 oxidation state.
  • Electron Configuration: A neutral Ni atom (Z=28) has the configuration [Ar] 3d⁸ 4s².
  • Ligand Field: CO is a very strong-field ligand. It will cause the electrons to rearrange and pair up. The 4s electrons will be pushed into the 3d orbitals.
  • Final Configuration: The 10 valence electrons (8 from 3d, 2 from 4s) will completely fill the 3d orbitals. The configuration becomes [Ar] 3d¹⁰ 4s⁰.
  • Magnetism: Since all 10 d-electrons are paired up in the 3d¹⁰ configuration, the complex has no unpaired electrons. This makes it diamagnetic, which matches the information given in the question.
  • Hybridization and Geometry: To accommodate the 4 CO ligands, the empty 4s and three 4p orbitals will hybridize. This results in sp³ hybridization and a tetrahedral geometry.

Analysis of [NiCl₄]²⁻

  • Oxidation State: The chloride ligand (Cl⁻) has a -1 charge. Let the oxidation state of Ni be x. Then x + 4(-1) = -2. This gives x = +2. Ni is in the +2 oxidation state.
  • Electron Configuration: A Ni²⁺ ion has lost its two 4s electrons. The configuration is [Ar] 3d⁸.
  • Magnetism: The question states the complex is paramagnetic. This means it must have unpaired electrons.
  • Ligand Field and Geometry: Cl⁻ is a weak-field ligand. It does not have enough strength to force the d-electrons to pair up. To accommodate the 4 Cl⁻ ligands, the Ni²⁺ ion will use its outer 4s and 4p orbitals for bonding. This leads to sp³ hybridization and a tetrahedral geometry. In this tetrahedral field, the 3d⁸ configuration will have two unpaired electrons, which explains its paramagnetism.
  • (What if it were square planar? A square planar geometry (dsp²) would require one d-orbital to be empty. To achieve this, the 8 d-electrons would have to pair up, leaving no unpaired electrons. This would make the complex diamagnetic, which contradicts the question.)

Summary of Findings:

  • [NiCl₄]²⁻: Ni(II), d⁸, paramagnetic, sp³, tetrahedral.
  • [Ni(CO)₄]: Ni(0), d¹⁰, diamagnetic, sp³, tetrahedral.
