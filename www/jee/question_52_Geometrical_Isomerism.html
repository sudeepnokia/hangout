Learning Guide: Geometrical Isomerism (Cis/Trans)

This guide explains what geometrical isomers are, the conditions required for them to exist, and their properties.

Phase 1: The Key Condition for Geometrical Isomerism

Geometrical isomerism (also known as cis-trans isomerism) is a type of stereoisomerism. It occurs when there is restricted rotation around a bond, usually a carbon-carbon double bond (C=C).

Because the atoms can't freely rotate around the double bond, the spatial arrangement of the groups attached to the carbons becomes fixed.

The Golden Rule: For a molecule with a C=C double bond to show geometrical isomerism, each of the two carbon atoms in the double bond must be attached to two different groups.

Let's look at a double bond: C₁=C₂. If C₁ is attached to groups A and B, and C₂ is attached to groups D and E, then for isomerism to exist:

  • A must be different from B.
  • D must be different from E.

Statement (A): Propene shows geometrical isomerism.

Statement (D): 2-methylbut-2-ene shows two geometrical isomers.

Question: Are statements (A) and (D) correct?

Analysis of Propene (CH₃-CH=CH₂):

Let's look at the carbons in the double bond (CH=CH₂).

  • The first carbon (CH) is attached to a methyl group (-CH₃) and a hydrogen (-H). These are different. So far, so good.
  • The second carbon (CH₂) is attached to two hydrogen atoms (-H and -H). These are identical!

Since the second carbon has two identical groups, propene fails the golden rule. It cannot show geometrical isomerism. Statement (A) is incorrect.

Analysis of 2-methylbut-2-ene (CH₃-C(CH₃)=CH-CH₃):

Let's look at the carbons in the double bond.

  • The first carbon is attached to two methyl groups (-CH₃ and -CH₃). These are identical!

It fails the rule immediately. 2-methylbut-2-ene cannot show geometrical isomerism. Statement (D) is incorrect.

Phase 2: Defining Cis and Trans

When a molecule *does* show geometrical isomerism, we can label the isomers as cis or trans.

Consider but-2-ene (CH₃-CH=CH-CH₃). Each carbon in the double bond is attached to a -H and a -CH₃ group. It meets the condition.

  • Cis isomer: The identical groups (e.g., the two -CH₃ groups) are on the same side of the double bond.
  • Trans isomer: The identical groups are on opposite sides (diagonally across) the double bond.

Statement (B): Trans isomer has identical atoms/groups on the opposite sides of the double bond.

Question: Is statement (B) correct?

This is the definition of a trans isomer. The prefix "trans-" means "across" or "on the other side of".

Therefore, Statement (B) is correct.

Phase 3: Dipole Moments and Melting Points

Because their shapes are different, cis and trans isomers have different physical properties.

Dipole Moment: A dipole moment arises from a separation of charge. In C-H and C-C bonds, there are small bond dipoles. We can treat them as vectors.

  • In trans-but-2-ene, the two C-CH₃ bond dipoles are opposite to each other and cancel out. The net dipole moment is zero (or very close to it).
  • In cis-but-2-ene, the two C-CH₃ bond dipoles point in roughly the same direction. They add up, resulting in a net dipole moment.

Melting Point: This is related to how well the molecules can pack into a solid crystal lattice.

  • The trans isomer is generally more symmetrical (straighter shape). It can pack more neatly and tightly into a crystal lattice. This leads to stronger intermolecular forces.
  • The cis isomer has a "kinked" shape and packs less efficiently.

Because of the better packing, it takes more energy to break the lattice of the trans isomer. Therefore, the trans isomer usually has a higher melting point than the cis isomer.

Statement (C): Cis-but-2-ene has higher dipole moment than trans-but-2-ene.

Statement (E): Trans-isomer has lower melting point than cis isomer.

Question: Are statements (C) and (E) correct?

Analysis of Statement (C): As explained, in cis-but-2-ene the bond dipoles add up, while in the trans isomer they cancel. So, the cis isomer has a significant dipole moment while the trans isomer has none. Statement (C) is correct.

Analysis of Statement (E): As explained, the more symmetrical trans isomer packs better in a crystal, leading to stronger forces and a higher melting point. The statement says the trans isomer has a *lower* melting point. Statement (E) is incorrect.
