Learning Guide: Understanding the Vernier Calliper

This guide explains the principle behind the Vernier calliper, a tool used for making very precise measurements.

Phase 1: Why Do We Need a Vernier Scale?

Imagine a regular ruler. The smallest marking is usually a millimeter (mm). You can measure something to the nearest millimeter, but you can't accurately measure something that is, say, 3.4 mm long. You would just be guessing the ".4" part.

A Vernier calliper solves this problem by using two scales:

  1. The Main Scale: This is just like a regular ruler, usually marked in millimeters.
  2. The Vernier Scale: This is a smaller, sliding scale that has markings that are slightly different in size from the main scale markings. This clever difference is what allows for precision measurements.

Phase 2: The Core Principle - Statement I

The magic of the Vernier scale comes from a deliberate mismatch in division size.

Typically, a certain number of divisions on the sliding Vernier scale (say, 10) are designed to match up perfectly with a slightly different number of divisions on the main scale (say, 9).

This means each division on the Vernier scale is slightly smaller than a division on the main scale.

Let's define:

  • 1 MSD: The value of one Main Scale Division (e.g., 1 mm).
  • 1 VSD: The value of one Vernier Scale Division.

If 10 VSDs line up with 9 MSDs, then 10 × 1 VSD = 9 × 1 MSD. This means 1 VSD = 0.9 MSD. As you can see, the Vernier scale division is indeed smaller than the main scale division.

Statement I: In a vernier callipers, one vernier scale division is always smaller than one main scale division.

Question: Is Statement I true or false?

As we saw in the concept, the fundamental design of a standard Vernier calliper requires the Vernier scale divisions (VSD) to be slightly smaller than the main scale divisions (MSD). This difference is what allows us to measure fractions of an MSD.

Therefore, Statement I is true.

(Note: While rare "retrograde" verniers exist where the VSD is larger, the standard instrument, and the one implied in physics problems unless stated otherwise, has VSD < MSD).

Phase 3: The Least Count - Statement II

The whole point of the Vernier calliper is to measure things smaller than one main scale division. The smallest possible measurement it can accurately make is called the Least Count (LC).

The Least Count is simply the difference in size between one main scale division and one vernier scale division.

Formula 1: LC = 1 MSD - 1 VSD

There is another, more practical way to calculate it. If 'n' is the number of divisions on the Vernier scale, the formula is:

Formula 2: LC = (Value of 1 MSD) / (Number of divisions on Vernier scale)

Statement II: The vernier constant is given by one main scale division multiplied by the number of vernier scale divisions.

Question: Is Statement II true or false? (Note: "Vernier constant" is another name for Least Count).

Let's analyze Statement II. It claims the formula is:

LC = 1 MSD × (Number of vernier scale divisions)

Now let's look at the correct formulas from our concept section:

Correct Formula 1: LC = 1 MSD - 1 VSD

Correct Formula 2: LC = 1 MSD / (Number of vernier scale divisions)

The formula in Statement II is clearly incorrect. It says to multiply by the number of VSDs, when we should be dividing.

Therefore, Statement II is false.

Phase 4: The Summit - The JEE Main Question

Original Question: Given below are two statements :

Statement II : The vernier constant is given by one main scale division multiplied by the number of vernier scale divisions.

In the light of the above statements, choose the correct answer from the options given below.

Let's review our findings from the previous phases.

Analysis of Statement I: We concluded that for a standard Vernier calliper, the VSD must be smaller than the MSD to work. So, Statement I is true.

Analysis of Statement II: We concluded that the formula for the Least Count (Vernier Constant) is (1 MSD) / (number of VSDs), not multiplied. So, Statement II is false.

We are looking for the option that says Statement I is true and Statement II is false.
