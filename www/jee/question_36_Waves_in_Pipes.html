Learning Guide: Sound Waves in Organ Pipes

This guide will teach you how sound behaves in different types of tubes, building up all the knowledge needed to solve a complex JEE problem.

Phase 1: How Fast is Sound?

The speed of sound isn't constant. It changes depending on the medium it's traveling through. For gases, two properties are very important:

  1. Bulk Modulus (B): Think of this as the gas's "stiffness". A stiffer gas snaps back into place faster, letting sound travel faster.
  2. Density (ρ): This is the "heaviness" of the gas. A denser, heavier gas is harder to move, so sound travels slower.

The formula that connects them is: v = √(B / ρ), where v is the speed of sound.

Warm-up Question: Gas 1 has a density ρ₁. Gas 2 is much lighter, with a density ρ₂ that is 1/16th of ρ₁. If they both have the same stiffness (Bulk Modulus B), how much faster is the speed of sound in Gas 2 (v₂) compared to Gas 1 (v₁)?

Let's write down the ratio of the speeds:

v₂ / v₁ = √(B / ρ₂) / √(B / ρ₁)

The B's cancel out, and we get: v₂ / v₁ = √(ρ₁ / ρ₂)

We are given ρ₁ / ρ₂ = 16 / 1 (since ρ₂ is 1/16 of ρ₁).

v₂ / v₁ = √(16) = 4

So, sound travels 4 times faster in the lighter gas (Gas 2).

Phase 2: A Step Up - Harmonics in an Open Pipe

An "open pipe" is a tube that is open at both ends. When you make a sound in it, special "standing waves" can form. These are called harmonics.

The frequency (pitch) of these harmonics depends on the pipe's length (L) and the speed of sound (v) in the gas inside.

The formula for the frequency of the n-th harmonic in an open pipe is:

f_n = n * (v / 2L), where n can be any whole number (1, 2, 3, ...).

Question: An open pipe of length L_o is filled with a gas where the speed of sound is v₂. What is the frequency of its 4th harmonic?

We use the formula with n = 4.

f_open = 4 * (v₂ / 2L_o) = 2 * v₂ / L_o

Phase 3: A Different Tune - Harmonics in a Closed Pipe

A "closed pipe" is open at one end and sealed at the other. This changes which standing waves can fit inside.

A closed pipe is pickier! It only allows odd-numbered harmonics.

The formula for the frequency of the n-th harmonic in a closed pipe is:

f_n = n * (v / 4L), where n MUST be an odd number (1, 3, 5, 7, ...).

Question: A closed pipe is 10 cm long and is filled with a gas where the speed of sound is v₁. What is the frequency of its 9th harmonic?

First, convert the length to meters: L_c = 10 cm = 0.1 m.

We use the formula with n = 9 (which is an odd number, so it's allowed).

f_closed = 9 * (v₁ / (4 * L_c)) = 9 * v₁ / (4 * 0.1) = 9 * v₁ / 0.4

Phase 4: The Summit - The JEE Main Question

Original Question: A closed organ and an open organ tube are filled by two different gases having same bulk modulus but different densities ρ₁ and ρ₂, respectively. The frequency of 9th harmonic of closed tube is identical with 4th harmonic of open tube. If the length of the closed tube is 10 cm and the density ratio of the gases is ρ₁ : ρ₂ = 1 : 16, then the length of the open tube is:

We have all the concepts. Let's put them together.

Step 1: Set the frequencies equal.

The problem states that the frequency of the 9th harmonic of the closed pipe is identical to the 4th harmonic of the open pipe.

f_closed = f_open

From our work in Phase 2 and 3:

9 * v₁ / 0.4 = 2 * v₂ / L_o

Step 2: Relate the speeds v₁ and v₂.

The gases are different. Gas 1 (in the closed pipe) has density ρ₁. Gas 2 (in the open pipe) has density ρ₂.

We are given ρ₁ / ρ₂ = 1 / 16.

From Phase 1, we know the relationship between speed and density. Let's find the ratio v₁/v₂:

v₁ / v₂ = √(B / ρ₁) / √(B / ρ₂) = √(ρ₂ / ρ₁)

v₁ / v₂ = √(16 / 1) = 4

This means v₁ = 4v₂. Sound is 4 times faster in the denser gas (Gas 1) than the lighter one (Gas 2). Wait, that's backwards! Let's re-read Phase 1. Denser gas = slower sound. Let's re-check the ratio.

ρ₁ / ρ₂ = 1 / 16 means ρ₂ = 16ρ₁. Gas 2 is 16 times denser.

v₁ / v₂ = √(ρ₂ / ρ₁) = √(16ρ₁ / ρ₁) = √(16) = 4. So v₁ = 4v₂. Correct. The sound is faster in the less dense gas (Gas 1).

Step 3: Substitute and solve for the unknown length (L_o).

Take our equation from Step 1 and replace v₁ with 4v₂:

9 * (4v₂) / 0.4 = 2 * v₂ / L_o

The speed v₂ appears on both sides, so we can cancel it out!

36 / 0.4 = 2 / L_o

Let's simplify the left side: 36 / 0.4 = 360 / 4 = 90.

90 = 2 / L_o

Now, rearrange to solve for L_o:

L_o = 2 / 90 = 1 / 45 meters.

Step 4: Convert the answer to centimeters.

The question asks for the length, and the options are in cm. There are 100 cm in a meter.

L_o = (1 / 45) * 100 cm = 100 / 45 cm

Simplify the fraction by dividing the top and bottom by 5:

L_o = 20 / 9 cm.

Final Answer Verification

Derived Answer: The length of the open tube is 20/9 cm.

Official Answer Key: The correct option for question 36 is (4), which corresponds to 20/9 cm.

Result: Match! Our step-by-step derivation is correct.
