Learning Guide: The Nature of Radioactive Decay

This guide explains the fundamental principles of radioactive decay, focusing on the decay constant and half-life.

Phase 1: The Random Nature of Decay

Radioactive decay is the process by which an unstable atomic nucleus loses energy by radiation. This process is fundamentally random and spontaneous at the level of a single atom. We can never predict when a specific nucleus will decay.

Because it is a nuclear process, it is not affected by external physical or chemical conditions like temperature, pressure, or chemical bonding. The forces inside the nucleus are immensely stronger than the forces that govern chemical reactions or thermal vibrations.

The rate of decay depends only on the type of nucleus and the number of nuclei present.

Statement (1): Decay constant increases with increase in temperature.

Statement (3): Decay constant does not depend upon temperature.

Question: Which of these statements is correct?

As explained in the concept, radioactive decay is a nuclear phenomenon, independent of external conditions like temperature.

The decay constant (λ) is a measure of the probability of decay per unit time for a single nucleus. Since the process itself is independent of temperature, the decay constant must also be independent of temperature.

Therefore, Statement (1) is false, and Statement (3) is correct.

Phase 2: Half-Life and the Decay Equation

Since we can't predict the decay of a single atom, we talk about the half-life (t½). This is the time it takes for half of the radioactive nuclei in a sample to decay.

The number of radioactive nuclei remaining (N) after a certain time (t) is given by:

N = N₀ * (1/2)^(t / t½)

  • N₀ is the initial number of nuclei.
  • t is the time elapsed.
  • t½ is the half-life.

Let's see what happens after 1, 2, and 3 half-lives:

  • After 1 half-life (t = t½): N = N₀ * (1/2)¹ = N₀/2 (Half remains)
  • After 2 half-lives (t = 2t½): N = N₀ * (1/2)² = N₀/4 (A quarter remains)
  • After 3 half-lives (t = 3t½): N = N₀ * (1/2)³ = N₀/8 (An eighth remains)

Statement (2): Amount of radioactive substance remained after three half lives is 1/8 th of original amount.

Question: Is this statement correct?

Using the formula or the logic from the concept section, after three half-lives, the amount remaining is:

N = N₀ * (1/2)³ = N₀ * (1/8)

So, 1/8th of the original amount remains. Statement (2) is correct.

Phase 3: The Link Between λ and t½

The decay constant (λ) and the half-life (t½) are two ways of describing the same decay process. They are related by a simple formula derived from the exponential decay law (N = N₀e^(-λt)).

The Relationship: t½ = ln(2) / λ

Where ln(2) is the natural logarithm of 2, which is approximately 0.693.

We can rearrange this to be: λ = ln(2) / t½ or t½ * λ = ln(2).

Statement (4): Half life is ln 2 times of 1/rate constant.

Question: Is this statement correct? (Note: "rate constant" is another name for decay constant, λ).

The statement says: Half life = ln(2) * (1 / rate constant)

Let's write this in symbols: t½ = ln(2) * (1 / λ)

This can be rewritten as t½ = ln(2) / λ.

This is the exact formula that relates half-life and the decay constant. Statement (4) is correct.
