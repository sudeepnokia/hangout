Learning Guide: Gravitation & Superposition Principle

Welcome! This guide will walk you through all the concepts needed to solve a complex gravitation problem, starting from the very basics.

Phase 1: The Absolute Basics - Force Between Two Points

Imagine two objects in space. They pull on each other with a force called gravity. Sir Isaac Newton figured out a way to calculate this force.

Newton's Law of Universal Gravitation: The force (F) between two point masses (m₁ and m₂) is directly proportional to the product of their masses and inversely proportional to the square of the distance (r) between their centers.

The formula is: F = G * (m₁ * m₂) / r²

Here, G is a special number called the Gravitational Constant. We don't need its exact value for this problem, just the relationship.

Warm-up Question: A large object of mass M and a small object of mass m are separated by a distance of 2R. What is the gravitational force between them?

We just use the formula directly!

m₁ = M

m₂ = m

r = 2R

F = G * (M * m) / (2R)² = G * M * m / 4R²

This is the force F₁ mentioned in the main problem.

Phase 2: A Step Up - The Sphere Trick

What if one of the objects isn't a tiny point, but a big, solid sphere (like a planet)? Calculating the pull from every single particle in the sphere sounds complicated!

The Shell Theorem: Luckily, Newton also proved something very convenient. For any point outside a uniform spherical shell or solid sphere, the sphere's gravitational pull is the exact same as if all of its mass were concentrated into a single point at its center.

Question: A uniform solid sphere has mass M and radius R. A point mass m is placed at a distance 2R from the sphere's center. What is the force on m?

Because of the Shell Theorem, we can pretend the entire mass M of the sphere is a single point at its center.

The distance from this "center point" to the mass m is 2R.

This is now the exact same as our Phase 1 problem!

F₁ = G * (M * m) / (2R)² = G M m / 4R²

This confirms our understanding of the initial force F₁.

Phase 3: The "Aha!" Moment - The Superposition Principle

How do we calculate the force from a sphere that has a piece missing? This is where a clever physics trick comes in, called the Principle of Superposition.

Imagine the sphere with the hole in it. The force from this object is hard to calculate directly.

Instead, let's think about it like this:

Force of (Sphere with Hole) = Force of (Complete Big Sphere) - Force of (The Small Sphere that was removed)

This is powerful! We already know how to calculate the force from complete spheres (Phase 2). So, all we need to do is calculate the force from the small piece that was "removed" and subtract it from the force of the original, complete sphere.

F₂ = F₁ - F_removed

Phase 4: Sizing Up the Missing Piece

To calculate the force from the removed piece, we first need its mass.

Density is Key: The sphere is uniform, meaning its density (mass per unit volume) is the same everywhere.

Density (ρ) = Total Mass / Total Volume

The volume of a sphere is V = (4/3)πr³.

Question: The big sphere has mass M and radius R. The removed spherical part has a radius of r_removed = R/3. What is the mass of the removed part (M_removed)?

Step 1: Find the density of the big sphere.

ρ = M / V = M / ((4/3)πR³)

Step 2: Find the volume of the removed sphere.

V_removed = (4/3)π * (r_removed)³ = (4/3)π * (R/3)³ = (4/3)π * (R³/27)

Step 3: Calculate the mass of the removed sphere.

M_removed = Density * V_removed

M_removed = [M / ((4/3)πR³)] * [(4/3)π * (R³/27)]

The (4/3)πR³ parts cancel out!

M_removed = M / 27

Phase 5: The Summit - The JEE Main Question

Original Question: A small point of mass m is placed at a distance 2R from the centre 'O' of a big uniform solid sphere of mass M and radius R. The gravitational force on 'm' due to M is F₁. A spherical part of radius R/3 is removed from the big sphere as shown in the figure and the gravitational force on m due to remaining part of M is found to be F₂. The value of ratio F₁ : F₂ is:

(The figure shows the removed sphere's center is on the line between 'O' and 'm'. Based on the problem's context and solution, the center of the removed sphere is located at a distance of 2R/3 from the main center O.)

We have all the pieces. Let's assemble the solution.

Step 1: Write down the force from the original, complete sphere (F₁).

From Phase 2, we know: F₁ = G M m / (2R)² = G M m / 4R²

Step 2: Calculate the force from the removed part (F_removed).

From Phase 4, the mass of the removed part is M_removed = M/27.

The center of the removed sphere is at a distance d = 2R/3 from O.

The point mass m is at a distance 2R from O.

So, the distance between the center of the removed sphere and mass m is r' = 2R - d = 2R - 2R/3 = 4R/3.

Now, we use the force formula for this removed sphere:

F_removed = G * M_removed * m / (r')²

F_removed = G * (M/27) * m / (4R/3)² = G * (M/27) * m / (16R²/9)

F_removed = (G M m / R²) * (9 / (27 * 16)) = (G M m / R²) * (1 / (3 * 16)) = G M m / 48R²

Step 3: Calculate the force from the sphere with the hole (F₂).

Using our superposition principle from Phase 3:

F₂ = F₁ - F_removed

F₂ = (G M m / 4R²) - (G M m / 48R²)

To subtract, we find a common denominator (48):

F₂ = (12 * G M m / 48R²) - (1 * G M m / 48R²) = 11 * G M m / 48R²

Step 4: Find the ratio F₁ : F₂.

F₁ / F₂ = (G M m / 4R²) / (11 G M m / 48R²)

The G M m / R² parts cancel out:

F₁ / F₂ = (1/4) / (11/48) = (1/4) * (48/11) = 48 / 44

Simplifying by dividing the top and bottom by 4:

F₁ / F₂ = 12 / 11

Final Answer Verification

Derived Answer: The ratio F₁ : F₂ is 12 : 11.
