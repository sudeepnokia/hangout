Learning Guide: Heat Flow and Thermal Resistance

This guide explains how heat flows through materials and introduces an analogy to electric circuits that makes solving complex problems much easier.

Phase 1: The Law of Heat Conduction

When there is a temperature difference across a material, heat will flow from the hotter end to the colder end. The rate of this heat flow (H) is also called heat current.

Fourier's Law of Heat Conduction: H = dQ/dt = kA(ΔT / L)

  • H: Rate of heat flow (in Watts or J/s).
  • k: Thermal conductivity of the material. A high 'k' means it's a good conductor of heat (like metal).
  • A: The cross-sectional area through which the heat is flowing.
  • L: The length of the material the heat is flowing through.
  • ΔT: The temperature difference between the hot and cold ends.

Phase 2: A Powerful Analogy - Thermal Resistance

The heat flow equation looks a lot like Ohm's Law for electric circuits, I = V/R.

Let's rearrange the heat equation: H = ΔT / (L / kA).

We can draw a direct comparison:

  • Heat Current (H) is like Electric Current (I).
  • Temperature Difference (ΔT) is like Voltage Difference (V).
  • The term (L / kA) is like Electrical Resistance (R).

We call this term the Thermal Resistance (R_th).

Thermal Resistance Formula: R_th = L / kA

This analogy is incredibly useful. We can now solve heat flow problems by drawing them as electrical circuits. Conductors in series and parallel behave just like resistors!

Warm-up Question: You have two rods of the same length and area. Rod 1 has conductivity k₁ and Rod 2 has conductivity k₂. What is the ratio of their thermal resistances, R₁/R₂?

R₁ = L / (k₁A)

R₂ = L / (k₂A)

R₁ / R₂ = (L / k₁A) / (L / k₂A)

The L and A terms cancel, leaving: R₁ / R₂ = k₂ / k₁.

Resistance is inversely proportional to conductivity, which makes sense!

Phase 3: Steady State and Junctions

When a system is in steady state, it means the temperatures at all points are no longer changing. The heat flowing *into* any junction must equal the heat flowing *out* of it.

This is exactly like Kirchhoff's Current Law for electrical junctions: the total current entering a junction equals the total current leaving it.

H_in = H_out

Phase 4: The Summit - The JEE Main Question

Original Question: Three conductors of same length having thermal conductivity k₁, k₂ and k₃ are connected as shown in figure. Area of cross sections of 1st and 2nd conductor are same and for 3rd conductor it is double of the 1st conductor. The temperatures are given in the figure. In steady state condition, the value of θ is ___ °C.

(The figure shows conductors 1 and 2 in parallel, and this combination is in series with conductor 3. The left end is at 100°C, the junction between the parallel pair and the third conductor is at θ, and the right end is at 0°C.)

Let's model this as a resistor circuit.

Step 1: Define the thermal resistances.

  • Length is the same for all: L.
  • Area of 1: A. Area of 2: A. Area of 3: 2A.
  • R₁ = L / (k₁A)
  • R₂ = L / (k₂A)
  • R₃ = L / (k₃ * 2A)

Step 2: Analyze the circuit diagram.

Resistors R₁ and R₂ are in parallel. Their equivalent resistance, R_p, is:

1/R_p = 1/R₁ + 1/R₂ = k₁A/L + k₂A/L = (k₁ + k₂)A / L

R_p = L / ((k₁ + k₂)A)

This parallel combination is in series with R₃. The total resistance is R_total = R_p + R₃.

Step 3: Apply the steady state condition.

The heat current flowing from the 100°C end to the junction at θ must be the same as the heat current flowing from θ to the 0°C end.

H_left = H_right

(T_hot - θ) / R_p = (θ - T_cold) / R₃

(100 - θ) / (L / ((k₁ + k₂)A)) = (θ - 0) / (L / (2k₃A))

Step 4: Simplify and solve for θ.

The terms L and A cancel from the denominators of both sides.

(100 - θ) * (k₁ + k₂) = θ * (2k₃)

100(k₁ + k₂) - θ(k₁ + k₂) = 2k₃θ

100(k₁ + k₂) = θ(k₁ + k₂ + 2k₃)

θ = 100 * (k₁ + k₂) / (k₁ + k₂ + 2k₃)

Step 5: Plug in the given values for k.

  • k₁ = 60
  • k₂ = 120
  • k₃ = 135

θ = 100 * (60 + 120) / (60 + 120 + 2*135)

θ = 100 * (180) / (180 + 270)

θ = 100 * 180 / 450

Simplify the fraction: 180 / 450 = 18 / 45 = 2 / 5 (dividing by 9).

θ = 100 * (2 / 5) = 200 / 5 = 40.

Final Answer Verification

Derived Answer: The temperature of the junction θ is 40 °C.

Official Answer Key: The correct answer for question 49 is 40.

Result: Match! Our step-by-step derivation is correct.
