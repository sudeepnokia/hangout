Learning Guide: Blackbody Radiation

This guide explains how hot objects radiate energy and how we can compare the energy radiated by different objects.

Phase 1: Hot Things Glow - The Stefan-Boltzmann Law

Any object with a temperature above absolute zero radiates energy into its surroundings. A hot stove, the sun, and even you are all radiating energy right now!

The rate at which an object radiates energy (its Power, P) is described by the Stefan-Boltzmann Law.

Formula: P = εσAT⁴

  • P: Power, or Energy radiated per second.
  • ε (epsilon): Emissivity. A number from 0 to 1 that describes how good an object is at radiating. We assume it's the same for similar materials.
  • σ (sigma): The Stefan-Boltzmann constant. It's just a number, and we won't need its value for this problem.
  • A: The surface area of the object. A bigger object has more surface to radiate from.
  • T: The absolute temperature of the object in Kelvin (K). This is the most important part - the power depends on the temperature to the fourth power!
Warm-up Question: If you double the absolute temperature of an object, how much more energy does it radiate per second?

The power is proportional to T⁴.

If the new temperature T' = 2T, the new power is P' ∝ (2T)⁴ = 16T⁴.

The new power is 16 times the original power. A small change in temperature leads to a huge change in radiated energy!

Phase 2: The Power of Ratios

When we need to compare two objects, we can use ratios. This is a great trick to cancel out all the constants (like ε and σ) that are the same for both objects.

Let's say we have two objects, 1 and 2. The ratio of their radiated power is:

P₂ / P₁ = (εσA₂T₂⁴) / (εσA₁T₁⁴)

Since ε and σ are the same for both, they cancel out:

P₂ / P₁ = (A₂ / A₁) * (T₂ / T₁)⁴

This simplified formula is all we need!

Phase 3: Sizing Up the Objects

The problem involves spherical bodies. The surface area (A) of a sphere with radius (r) is given by the formula:

A = 4πr²

Question: One sphere has a radius of 0.8 m. Another has a radius of 0.2 m. What is the ratio of their surface areas (A_bigger / A_smaller)?

A_bigger = 4π(0.8)²

A_smaller = 4π(0.2)²

A_bigger / A_smaller = (4π(0.8)²) / (4π(0.2)²)

The 4π cancels out.

Ratio = (0.8 / 0.2)² = (4)² = 16

The bigger sphere has 16 times the surface area of the smaller one.

Phase 4: The Summit - The JEE Main Question

Original Question: Two spherical bodies of same materials having radii 0.2 m and 0.8 m are placed in same atmosphere. The temperature of the smaller body is 800 K and temperature of the bigger body is 400 K. If the energy radiated from the smaller body is E, the energy radiated from the bigger body is:

Let's identify our variables. Let the smaller body be object 1 and the bigger body be object 2.

  • Radius 1 (smaller): r₁ = 0.2 m
  • Temperature 1 (smaller): T₁ = 800 K
  • Energy of smaller body: E₁ = E
  • Radius 2 (bigger): r₂ = 0.8 m
  • Temperature 2 (bigger): T₂ = 400 K
  • Energy of bigger body: E₂ = ?

We use our ratio formula from Phase 2:

E₂ / E₁ = (A₂ / A₁) * (T₂ / T₁)⁴

Step 1: Calculate the area ratio (A₂ / A₁).

A₂ / A₁ = (4πr₂²) / (4πr₁²) = (r₂ / r₁)²

A₂ / A₁ = (0.8 / 0.2)² = (4)² = 16

Step 2: Calculate the temperature ratio (T₂ / T₁).

T₂ / T₁ = 400 / 800 = 1/2

Step 3: Plug the ratios into the main formula.

E₂ / E₁ = (16) * (1/2)⁴

E₂ / E₁ = 16 * (1 / 16)

E₂ / E₁ = 1

Step 4: Find E₂.

Since E₂ / E₁ = 1, this means E₂ = E₁.

The problem states the energy from the smaller body is E, so E₁ = E.

Therefore, E₂ = E. The energy radiated from the bigger body is also E.

Final Answer Verification

Derived Answer: The energy radiated from the bigger body is E.

Official Answer Key: The correct option for question 41 is (2), which corresponds to E.
