Learning Guide: Electrolysis and Peroxydisulfuric Acid

This guide explains the principles of electrolysis and how it can be used to synthesize specific chemical compounds.

Phase 1: What is Electrolysis?

Electrolysis is the process of using a direct electric current (DC) to drive an otherwise non-spontaneous chemical reaction. It is carried out in an electrolytic cell, which contains two electrodes (anode and cathode) submerged in an electrolyte.

Key Definitions:

  • Electrolyte: A substance that produces an electrically conducting solution when dissolved in a polar solvent, such as water. It contains ions.
  • Anode: The positive electrode. Oxidation occurs here (loss of electrons). Anions move towards the anode.
  • Cathode: The negative electrode. Reduction occurs here (gain of electrons). Cations move towards the cathode.

In the electrolysis of an aqueous solution, you must consider not only the ions from the salt but also the water itself, which can be oxidized (to O₂) or reduced (to H₂).

Phase 2: Special Types of Electrolysis

Certain electrolysis reactions are famous for the specific products they create.

Kolbe Electrolysis: The electrolysis of an aqueous solution of a sodium or potassium salt of a carboxylic acid. It results in the formation of an alkane at the anode.

Marshall's Acid Synthesis: This is a specific industrial process to produce peroxydisulfuric acid (H₂S₂O₈), also known as Marshall's acid. It involves the electrolysis of a cold, concentrated solution of sulfuric acid (H₂SO₄) or an alkali metal bisulfate (like KHSO₄) at a high current density.

At the anode, the bisulfate ions (HSO₄⁻) or sulfate ions (SO₄²⁻) are oxidized and dimerize (pair up) to form the peroxydisulfate ion (S₂O₈²⁻).

Anode Reaction: 2HSO₄⁻ → S₂O₈²⁻ + 2H⁺ + 2e⁻

The resulting product in the electrolyte is peroxydisulfuric acid, H₂S₂O₈.

Phase 3: The Summit - The JEE Main Question

Original Question: Which of the following electrolyte can be used to obtain H₂S₂O₈ by the process of electrolysis?

  1. Dilute solution of sodium sulphate.
  2. Acidified dilute solution of sodium sulphate.
  3. Dilute solution of sulphuric acid.
  4. Concentrated solution of sulphuric acid.

The question is asking for the specific conditions required to produce H₂S₂O₈ (peroxydisulfuric acid).

From our concept in Phase 2, we know that this synthesis is a specific, named process called the synthesis of Marshall's acid.

The key requirements for this process are:

  1. The presence of sulfate (SO₄²⁻) or bisulfate (HSO₄⁻) ions.
  2. The solution must be cold and concentrated.
  3. A high current density is needed.

Let's analyze the options:

  1. Dilute solution of sodium sulphate: This has the right ions, but it is dilute. In a dilute aqueous solution, the oxidation of water to produce oxygen gas (2H₂O → O₂ + 4H⁺ + 4e⁻) is favored over the oxidation of sulfate ions. So, this will not work.
  2. Acidified dilute solution of sodium sulphate: Same problem as above. It is dilute, so water will be oxidized preferentially.
  3. Dilute solution of sulphuric acid: Again, the key issue is that the solution is dilute. Water will be oxidized instead of the sulfate ions.
  4. Concentrated solution of sulphuric acid: This matches the specific conditions required for the synthesis of Marshall's acid. In a concentrated solution, the concentration of water is much lower, and the concentration of bisulfate/sulfate ions is very high. This high concentration favors the oxidation of the sulfate species to form the peroxydisulfate ion at the anode.

Therefore, the correct electrolyte is a concentrated solution of sulphuric acid.

Final Answer Verification

Derived Answer: The correct electrolyte is a Concentrated solution of sulphuric acid.

Official Answer Key: The correct option for question 58 is (4), which corresponds to Concentrated solution of sulphuric acid.
