Learning Guide: Multi-Step Gas Stoichiometry

This guide breaks down a two-step reaction problem involving gases, neutralization, and the ideal gas law.

Phase 1: Neutralization Reactions

A neutralization reaction occurs when an acid and a base react to form a salt and water. The key to solving these problems is understanding the stoichiometry (the mole ratios).

The reaction between calcium hydroxide (a base) and hydrochloric acid (an acid) is:

Ca(OH)₂ + 2HCl → CaCl₂ + 2H₂O

This balanced equation tells us that 1 mole of Ca(OH)₂ reacts completely with 2 moles of HCl.

We can calculate moles of a solute if we know its molarity (M) and volume (V in Liters):

Moles = Molarity × Volume

Warm-up Question: How many moles of Ca(OH)₂ are needed to neutralize 40 mL of 0.1 M HCl?

Step 1: Find moles of HCl.

First, convert volume to Liters: 40 mL = 0.040 L.

Moles_HCl = 0.1 mol/L * 0.040 L = 0.004 moles.

Step 2: Use stoichiometry to find moles of Ca(OH)₂.

From the 1:2 ratio, Moles_Ca(OH)₂ = Moles_HCl / 2.

Moles_Ca(OH)₂ = 0.004 / 2 = 0.002 moles.

Phase 2: The Reaction of CO₂ with Ca(OH)₂

When carbon dioxide gas is bubbled through a solution of calcium hydroxide (limewater), it reacts to form a white precipitate of calcium carbonate.

CO₂(g) + Ca(OH)₂ (aq) → CaCO₃(s) + H₂O(l)

The stoichiometry here is simple: 1 mole of CO₂ reacts with 1 mole of Ca(OH)₂.

Phase 3: Relating Moles of Gas to Volume

The Ideal Gas Law relates the pressure (P), volume (V), number of moles (n), and temperature (T) of a gas.

Ideal Gas Law: PV = nRT

  • P: Pressure (in atm)
  • V: Volume (in Liters)
  • n: Moles
  • R: Ideal gas constant (0.0821 L·atm/mol·K)
  • T: Temperature (in Kelvin)

Phase 4: The Summit - The JEE Main Question

Original Question: Some CO₂ gas was kept in a sealed container at a pressure of 1 atm and at 273 K. This entire amount of CO₂ gas was later passed through an aqueous solution of Ca(OH)₂. The excess unreacted Ca(OH)₂ was later neutralized with 0.1 M of 40 mL HCl. If the volume of the sealed container of CO₂ was x, then x is ___ cm³. [Given: The entire amount of CO₂(g) reacted with exactly half the initial amount of Ca(OH)₂ present in the aqueous solution.]

This is a multi-step problem. Let's work backwards from the final step.

Step 1: Find the moles of excess Ca(OH)₂.

This is the amount of Ca(OH)₂ that was left over after reacting with CO₂. This amount was neutralized by the HCl. From our warm-up question in Phase 1, we already calculated this!

Moles of excess Ca(OH)₂ = 0.002 moles.

Step 2: Find the moles of Ca(OH)₂ that reacted with CO₂.

The problem states that the CO₂ reacted with "exactly half the initial amount of Ca(OH)₂". This means the other half was the "excess" amount we just calculated.

So, the amount that reacted must be equal to the amount that was in excess.

Moles of Ca(OH)₂ reacted with CO₂ = 0.002 moles.

Step 3: Find the moles of CO₂ that reacted.

From the reaction in Phase 2 (CO₂ + Ca(OH)₂ → CaCO₃), the mole ratio is 1:1.

Therefore, Moles of CO₂ = Moles of Ca(OH)₂ reacted = 0.002 moles.

Step 4: Use the Ideal Gas Law to find the volume of the CO₂.

Now we know the number of moles of CO₂ gas (n) that was in the container initially. We are given its pressure and temperature.

  • n = 0.002 mol
  • P = 1 atm
  • T = 273 K (Note: 273 K and 1 atm are STP conditions)
  • R = 0.0821 L·atm/mol·K

PV = nRT → V = nRT / P

V = (0.002 * 0.0821 * 273) / 1

V ≈ 0.0448 L

Alternatively, using the molar volume at STP (1 mole = 22.4 L):

V = 0.002 mol * 22.4 L/mol = 0.0448 L.

Step 5: Convert the volume to cm³.

1 Liter = 1000 milliliters (mL)

1 mL = 1 cm³

So, 1 Liter = 1000 cm³.

V = 0.0448 L * 1000 cm³/L = 44.8 cm³.

Step 6: Round to the nearest integer.

The nearest integer to 44.8 is 45.

Final Answer Verification

Derived Answer: The volume x is 45 cm³.

Official Answer Key: The correct answer for question 74 is 45.

Result: Match! Our step-by-step analysis is correct.
