Learning Guide: Capacitors, Energy, and Dielectrics

This guide explains how capacitors store charge and energy, and what happens when you insert a special material called a dielectric between their plates.

Phase 1: The Basics of a Capacitor

A capacitor is a device that stores electrical energy. The simplest version is a "parallel-plate capacitor," made of two metal plates separated by a gap (usually filled with air or another insulator).

Capacitance (C): This is a measure of how much charge a capacitor can store for a given voltage. A bigger capacitance means it can store more charge. The unit of capacitance is the Farad (F).

The Holy Trinity of Capacitor Equations:

  1. Charge: Q = C * V (The charge stored is capacitance times voltage)
  2. Energy: U = (1/2) * C * V² (The energy stored in the capacitor)
  3. Energy (alternative): U = Q² / (2C)
Warm-up Question: A capacitor with capacitance C = 40 μF is connected to a 100 V power supply. How much charge (Q) and energy (U) does it store?

First, let's make sure our units are standard. 1 μF = 10⁻⁶ F.

C = 40 * 10⁻⁶ F

V = 100 V

Charge:

Q = C * V = (40 * 10⁻⁶ F) * (100 V) = 4000 * 10⁻⁶ C

This is 4 * 10⁻³ C, which is also known as 4 milliCoulombs (4 mC).

Energy:

U = (1/2) * C * V² = (1/2) * (40 * 10⁻⁶ F) * (100 V)²

U = (20 * 10⁻⁶) * (10000) = 200000 * 10⁻⁶ J = 0.2 J

So, initially, the capacitor stores 4 mC of charge and 0.2 J of energy.

Phase 2: A Step Up - Introducing a Dielectric

A dielectric is a special insulating material that can be inserted between the capacitor plates. When you do this, it has a remarkable effect: it increases the capacitance.

The dielectric constant (K) tells you how much the capacitance is boosted. K is always greater than 1 (for air or a vacuum, K=1).

New Capacitance Formula: C_new = K * C_original

Question: Our 40 μF capacitor is filled with a dielectric material with a dielectric constant K = 2. What is its new capacitance?

C_original = 40 μF

K = 2

C_new = K * C_original = 2 * 40 μF = 80 μF

The capacitance has doubled.

Phase 3: What Happens Next? The Crucial Detail

When you insert a dielectric, what happens to the charge and energy depends on one critical thing: is the capacitor still connected to the battery, or has it been disconnected?

Scenario in this problem: The capacitor is connected to the power supply *while* the dielectric is inserted.

This means the Voltage (V) is held constant by the battery at 100 V. The capacitor is now able to store more charge, so the battery will supply extra charge to fill it up to its new, larger capacity.

Phase 4: The Summit - The JEE Main Question

Original Question: A parallel-plate capacitor of capacitance 40μF is connected to a 100 V power supply. Now the intermediate space between the plates is filled with a dielectric material of dielectric constant K = 2. Due to the introduction of dielectric material, the extra charge and the change in the electrostatic energy in the capacitor, respectively, are:

Let's calculate the final state and compare it to the initial state from Phase 1.

Initial State (from Phase 1):

  • C_initial = 40 μF
  • Q_initial = 4 mC
  • U_initial = 0.2 J

Final State (with dielectric, battery connected):

  • Voltage is constant: V_final = 100 V
  • New capacitance (from Phase 2): C_final = 80 μF

Step 1: Calculate the final charge (Q_final).

Q_final = C_final * V_final = (80 * 10⁻⁶ F) * (100 V) = 8000 * 10⁻⁶ C = 8 mC

Step 2: Calculate the extra charge supplied.

Extra Charge = Q_final - Q_initial = 8 mC - 4 mC = 4 mC

Step 3: Calculate the final energy (U_final).

U_final = (1/2) * C_final * V_final² = (1/2) * (80 * 10⁻⁶ F) * (100 V)²

U_final = (40 * 10⁻⁶) * (10000) = 400000 * 10⁻⁶ J = 0.4 J

Step 4: Calculate the change in energy.

Change in Energy = U_final - U_initial = 0.4 J - 0.2 J = 0.2 J

So, the extra charge is 4 mC and the change in energy is 0.2 J.
