Learning Guide: Counting Stereoisomers

This guide explains how to identify sources of stereoisomerism in a molecule and calculate the total number of possible stereoisomers.

Phase 1: What Creates Stereoisomers?

Stereoisomers are molecules that have the same molecular formula and the same connectivity of atoms, but differ in the three-dimensional arrangement of their atoms in space.

There are two main sources of stereoisomerism in organic molecules:

  1. Chiral Centers (or Chiral Carbons): A carbon atom that is bonded to four different groups. A chiral center can exist in two different 3D arrangements (called enantiomers), designated as R or S.
  2. Geometrical Isomerism (Cis/Trans): This occurs due to restricted rotation, usually around a carbon-carbon double bond. For a double bond to exhibit geometrical isomerism, each carbon in the bond must be attached to two different groups. This gives rise to cis/trans (or E/Z) isomers.

Phase 2: Analyzing the Molecule

Original Question: How many different stereoisomers are possible for the given molecule?

Molecule: CH₃-CH(OH)-CH=CH-CH₃ (Pentan-3-en-2-ol)

Let's examine the molecule to find all possible sources of stereoisomerism.

C₅H₃ - C₄H = C₃H - C₂(OH)H - C₁H₃

1. Check for Chiral Centers:

  • C1 (CH₃): Bonded to 3 H's. Not chiral.
  • C2 (CH(OH)): Let's see what it's bonded to:
    1. A hydrogen atom (-H)
    2. A hydroxyl group (-OH)
    3. A methyl group (-CH₃)
    4. A -CH=CH-CH₃ group.
    These four groups are all different. Therefore, C2 is a chiral center. This gives us one source of stereoisomerism (R/S).
  • C3 (CH=): Part of a double bond. Cannot be a chiral center.
  • C4 (=CH): Part of a double bond. Cannot be a chiral center.
  • C5 (CH₃): Bonded to 3 H's. Not chiral.

2. Check for Geometrical Isomerism:

  • The molecule has a double bond between C3 and C4. Let's check if it meets the condition.
  • C3 is bonded to: A hydrogen atom (-H) and a -CH(OH)-CH₃ group. These are different.
  • C4 is bonded to: A hydrogen atom (-H) and a methyl group (-CH₃). These are also different.
  • Since both carbons of the double bond are attached to two different groups, the double bond can exhibit geometrical isomerism (cis/trans or E/Z). This gives us a second source of stereoisomerism.

So, we have found two stereogenic elements in the molecule: one chiral carbon (C2) and one double bond capable of geometrical isomerism (C3=C4).

Phase 3: Calculating the Total Number of Stereoisomers

For a molecule with multiple, independent stereocenters, the total number of possible stereoisomers can be calculated with a simple formula.

If a molecule has 'n' stereogenic centers (this includes both chiral centers and double bonds that can show geometrical isomerism) and the molecule is unsymmetrical, the maximum number of stereoisomers is:

Max Stereoisomers = 2ⁿ

(This formula can have exceptions if the molecule is symmetrical, which can lead to meso compounds, but our molecule is not symmetrical).

Question: For the molecule CH₃-CH(OH)-CH=CH-CH₃, how many stereoisomers are possible?

From our analysis in Phase 2, we identified n = 2 stereogenic centers:

  1. The chiral carbon at C2.
  2. The double bond between C3 and C4.

The molecule is unsymmetrical, so we can use the formula 2ⁿ.

Total stereoisomers = 2ⁿ = 2² = 4.

Let's list them out to be sure:

  • (R)-configuration at C2, trans-double bond
  • (S)-configuration at C2, trans-double bond
  • (R)-configuration at C2, cis-double bond
  • (S)-configuration at C2, cis-double bond

These are four distinct molecules that cannot be superimposed on each other.
