Learning Guide: The Photoelectric Effect

This guide explains how light can knock electrons out of a metal, a phenomenon that puzzled early physicists and led to the birth of quantum mechanics.

Phase 1: Light as a Particle (Photon)

We often think of light as a wave, but Albert Einstein proposed that light can also behave like a stream of tiny energy packets called photons.

The energy of a single photon depends on the light's wavelength (λ) or frequency (f).

Photon Energy Formula: E = h * f = h * c / λ

  • E is the energy of one photon.
  • h is Planck's constant.
  • c is the speed of light.
  • λ is the wavelength of the light.

A very useful shortcut for calculations is the combined value hc ≈ 1240 eV·nm. This gives the energy in electron-volts (eV) if you plug in the wavelength in nanometers (nm).

Warm-up Question: A beam of light has a wavelength of 550 nm. What is the energy of a single photon in this beam, in electron-volts (eV)?

We use the handy shortcut formula:

E = 1240 eV·nm / λ

E = 1240 / 550 eV

E ≈ 2.25 eV

So, each photon in this light beam carries about 2.25 eV of energy.

Phase 2: The Work Function

Electrons in a metal are held inside it. To pull an electron out of the metal, you need to give it a certain minimum amount of energy. This "escape energy" is called the Work Function (Φ).

Every metal has its own unique work function. Some metals hold their electrons tightly (high work function), while others let them go more easily (low work function).

Think of it like an admission fee. If a photon hits the metal, it gives all its energy to one electron. If the photon's energy is less than the work function, the electron can't "pay the fee" and remains trapped inside.

Question: A metal has a work function of 2.5 eV. A photon with 2.25 eV of energy hits the metal. Will an electron be ejected?

Energy of incoming photon = 2.25 eV.

Energy required to escape (Work Function) = 2.5 eV.

Since 2.25 eV < 2.5 eV, the photon does not have enough energy to pay the escape fee. No electron will be ejected.

Phase 3: The Condition for the Photoelectric Effect

The photoelectric effect (the ejection of electrons by light) only happens if the energy of the incoming photon (E) is greater than or equal to the work function (Φ) of the metal.

Condition: E ≥ Φ

Or, using the photon energy formula:

(h * c / λ) ≥ Φ

Any extra energy the photon has beyond the work function becomes the kinetic energy (moving energy) of the ejected electron, but for this problem, we only need to know if the effect happens at all.

Phase 4: The Summit - The JEE Main Question

Original Question: The work functions of cesium (Cs) and lithium (Li) metals are 1.9 eV and 2.5 eV, respectively. If we incident a light of wavelength 550 nm on these two metal surfaces, then photo-electric effect is possible for the case of:

We need to check the condition from Phase 3 for each metal.

Step 1: Calculate the energy of the incoming photons.

From our warm-up question in Phase 1, we already did this!

The light has a wavelength λ = 550 nm.

The energy of each photon is E = 1240 / 550 ≈ 2.25 eV.

Step 2: Check the condition for Cesium (Cs).

Work function of Cesium, Φ_Cs = 1.9 eV.

Is E ≥ Φ_Cs?

Is 2.25 eV ≥ 1.9 eV? Yes, it is.

So, the photoelectric effect is possible for Cesium.

Step 3: Check the condition for Lithium (Li).

Work function of Lithium, Φ_Li = 2.5 eV.

Is E ≥ Φ_Li?

Is 2.25 eV ≥ 2.5 eV? No, it is not.

So, the photoelectric effect is not possible for Lithium.

Conclusion: The effect is only possible for Cesium.
