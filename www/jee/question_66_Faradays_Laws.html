Learning Guide: Faraday's Laws of Electrolysis

This guide explains how to calculate the amount of substance deposited during electrolysis using Faraday's laws.

Phase 1: Total Charge Passed

When a constant electric current (I) flows for a certain amount of time (t), the total electric charge (Q) that has passed is given by a very simple formula.

Charge Formula: Q = I × t

  • Q is the charge in Coulombs (C).
  • I is the current in Amperes (A).
  • t is the time in seconds (s).
Warm-up Question: A current of 2 A is passed through a solution for 30 minutes. What is the total charge passed in Coulombs?

First, we must convert the time to seconds.

t = 30 minutes * 60 seconds/minute = 1800 s

Now, use the formula:

Q = 2 A * 1800 s = 3600 C

Phase 2: The Faraday Constant

A single electron has a tiny charge. It's more convenient to talk about the charge of a mole of electrons (6.022 x 10²³ electrons). This amount of charge is called the Faraday constant (F).

Faraday Constant: F ≈ 96500 Coulombs per mole of electrons

This constant is the bridge between the macroscopic charge (Q) we measure in our circuit and the number of moles of electrons that are actually doing the work in the chemical reaction.

Moles of electrons (n_e): n_e = Q / F

Question: For the 3600 C of charge calculated in Phase 1, how many moles of electrons were passed?

n_e = Q / F = 3600 C / 96500 C/mol

n_e ≈ 0.0373 moles of electrons

Phase 3: From Electrons to Metal

The final step is to relate the moles of electrons to the moles of metal being deposited. This requires looking at the half-reaction for the reduction of the metal ion.

For the electrolysis of aluminium chloride (AlCl₃), the aluminium is deposited at the cathode. The ion is Al³⁺.

Cathode Half-Reaction: Al³⁺ + 3e⁻ → Al(s)

This equation tells us the crucial stoichiometric ratio: 3 moles of electrons are required to deposit 1 mole of solid aluminium.

Phase 4: The Summit - The JEE Main Question

Original Question: A solution of aluminium chloride is electrolysed for 30 minutes using a current of 2 A. The amount of the aluminium deposited at the cathode is [Given: molar mass of aluminium is 27 g mol⁻¹. Faraday constant = 96500 C mol⁻¹]

Let's combine all the steps into one calculation. This is Faraday's First Law of Electrolysis.

Step 1: Calculate the total charge (Q).

As in Phase 1, Q = I * t = 2 A * (30 * 60 s) = 3600 C.

Step 2: Calculate the moles of electrons (n_e).

As in Phase 2, n_e = Q / F = 3600 / 96500 mol.

Step 3: Calculate the moles of Aluminium deposited (n_Al).

From the stoichiometry in Phase 3 (Al³⁺ + 3e⁻ → Al), we know that moles of Al = (moles of e⁻) / 3.

n_Al = n_e / 3 = (3600 / 96500) / 3 = 1200 / 96500 mol.

Step 4: Calculate the mass of Aluminium deposited.

Mass = Moles × Molar Mass

Mass_Al = n_Al * MolarMass_Al

Mass_Al = (1200 / 96500) * 27 g

Mass_Al = 32400 / 96500 g

Mass_Al ≈ 0.3357 g

Let's check the options. 0.336 g is one of the options.

A more compact way to write this (Faraday's Law):

Mass = (I * t / F) * (Molar Mass / n-factor)

Where the n-factor is the number of electrons in the half-reaction (n=3 for Al³⁺).

Mass = (2 * 1800 / 96500) * (27 / 3)

Mass = (3600 / 96500) * 9 = 32400 / 96500 ≈ 0.336 g
