Learning Guide: How Temperature Affects Resistance

This guide explains how the electrical resistance of different types of materials changes as they get hotter or colder.

Phase 1: What is Resistivity?

Resistance (R) is a measure of how much a specific object (like a piece of wire) opposes the flow of electric current. It's measured in Ohms (Ω).

Resistivity (ρ) is a more fundamental property of a material itself. It tells you how resistive that substance is, regardless of its shape or size. A material with low resistivity is a good conductor (like copper), and a material with high resistivity is a poor conductor (like rubber).

The question asks about resistivity (ρ) versus temperature (T), which is a fundamental material property.

Phase 2: Conductors - The Traffic Jam Analogy

In a metal conductor, electrons flow freely. Think of them as cars on a highway.

When you increase the temperature, the metal atoms themselves start vibrating more and more violently. These vibrating atoms get in the way of the flowing electrons, causing more "collisions".

It's like adding random, shaking obstacles to the highway. The flow of traffic (current) is obstructed more. Therefore, for conductors:

As Temperature (↑), Resistivity (↑).

The relationship is nearly linear for a wide range of temperatures. The graph of ρ vs. T is a line that slopes upwards.

Phase 3: Semiconductors - The Reluctant Employee Analogy

In a semiconductor (like silicon), electrons are not as free as in a metal. They need an energy boost to break free and conduct electricity.

When you increase the temperature, you are giving these electrons the energy they need to break free and become charge carriers. More heat means more free electrons available to carry current.

It's like an office where employees need a lot of coffee (energy) to start working. More coffee (heat) means more employees get up and start moving (conducting).

Even though the collisions from Phase 2 also increase, the effect of creating more charge carriers is much more dominant. Therefore, for semiconductors:

As Temperature (↑), Resistivity (↓).

The relationship is not linear; it's an exponential decay. The graph of ρ vs. T is a curve that slopes downwards.

Phase 4: The Summit - The JEE Main Question

Original Question: Which of the following resistivity (ρ) v/s temperature (T) curves is most suitable to be used in wire bound standard resistors?

Let's think about what makes a good "standard resistor". A standard resistor is used as a reference in circuits. You want its resistance to be reliable and predictable. If you build a circuit with a 100 Ω standard resistor, you want it to be 100 Ω whether your room is cold or hot.

This means the ideal material for a standard resistor is one whose resistivity changes very little with temperature.

Let's analyze the graphs provided in the question:

  • Graph (1): Shows resistivity decreasing sharply as temperature increases. This is the behavior of a semiconductor. Not good for a standard resistor.
  • Graph (2): Shows resistivity increasing sharply and linearly as temperature increases. This is the behavior of a typical metal conductor. Also not ideal, as its resistance would change a lot with temperature.
  • Graph (3): Shows resistivity decreasing, similar to a semiconductor. Not suitable.
  • Graph (4): Shows a material that has a certain resistivity, and as the temperature increases, that resistivity barely changes at all. The line is almost flat.

This fourth graph represents the ideal behavior for a standard resistor. We want a material that is insensitive to temperature changes.

Materials like this exist! They are special alloys, such as Manganin or Constantan, which are specifically engineered to have a very low temperature coefficient of resistivity. They are used to make high-precision, wire-wound standard resistors.
