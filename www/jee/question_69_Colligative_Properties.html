Learning Guide: Boiling Point Elevation

This guide explains why the boiling point of a solvent increases when you dissolve a solute in it, and how to rank solutions by their boiling points.

Phase 1: What are Colligative Properties?

Colligative properties are properties of solutions that depend on the number of solute particles in a solution, not on the identity of the solute particles.

One important colligative property is Boiling Point Elevation. When you dissolve a non-volatile solute (like salt or sugar) into a solvent (like water), the boiling point of the resulting solution will be higher than the boiling point of the pure solvent.

The more particles you dissolve, the greater the increase in the boiling point.

Phase 2: Counting Particles in Solution

To compare colligative properties, we need to know the total concentration of all particles in the solution. This is where the van't Hoff factor (i) comes in.

The van't Hoff factor tells us how many particles a solute breaks into when it dissolves.

  • For non-electrolytes (covalent compounds like urea or sugar that don't break apart into ions), i = 1. One molecule goes in, one particle stays in solution.
  • For electrolytes (ionic compounds like NaCl that dissociate into ions), i is the number of ions formed per formula unit.
Warm-up Question: What is the van't Hoff factor (i) for NaCl and for Urea (NH₂CONH₂)?

NaCl: This is an ionic compound. When it dissolves, it breaks into two ions: NaCl(s) → Na⁺(aq) + Cl⁻(aq). So, for NaCl, i = 2.

Urea: This is a covalent molecule. It dissolves in water but does not break apart into smaller particles. So, for Urea, i = 1.

Phase 3: The Summit - The JEE Main Question

Original Question: Arrange the following solutions in order of their increasing boiling points.

(i) 10⁻⁴M NaCl

(ii) 10⁻⁴M Urea

(iii) 10⁻³M NaCl

(iv) 10⁻²M NaCl

The elevation in boiling point is directly proportional to the effective particle concentration. We can calculate this by multiplying the molarity (M) by the van't Hoff factor (i).

Effective Concentration = i × M

A higher effective concentration will lead to a higher boiling point. Let's calculate this value for each solution.

(i) 10⁻⁴M NaCl:

  • M = 10⁻⁴
  • i = 2 (since NaCl → Na⁺ + Cl⁻)
  • Effective Concentration = 2 × 10⁻⁴

(ii) 10⁻⁴M Urea:

  • M = 10⁻⁴
  • i = 1 (Urea is a non-electrolyte)
  • Effective Concentration = 1 × 10⁻⁴

(iii) 10⁻³M NaCl:

  • M = 10⁻³ = 10 × 10⁻⁴
  • i = 2
  • Effective Concentration = 2 × 10⁻³ = 20 × 10⁻⁴

(iv) 10⁻²M NaCl:

  • M = 10⁻² = 100 × 10⁻⁴
  • i = 2
  • Effective Concentration = 2 × 10⁻² = 200 × 10⁻⁴

Step 2: Rank the effective concentrations from lowest to highest.

  • Lowest: (ii) = 1 × 10⁻⁴
  • Next: (i) = 2 × 10⁻⁴
  • Next: (iii) = 20 × 10⁻⁴
  • Highest: (iv) = 200 × 10⁻⁴

The order of increasing effective concentration is: (ii) < (i) < (iii) < (iv).

Since boiling point elevation is proportional to this concentration, this is also the order of increasing boiling points.
