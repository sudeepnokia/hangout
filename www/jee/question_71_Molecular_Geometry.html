Learning Guide: Predicting Molecular Geometry with VSEPR

This guide explains how to use the VSEPR model to predict the shape of molecules and identify which ones are linear.

Phase 1: VSEPR Theory

The Valence Shell Electron Pair Repulsion (VSEPR) theory is a model used to predict the 3D geometry of individual molecules. The main idea is that electron pairs in the valence shell of a central atom will repel each other and arrange themselves to be as far apart as possible.

To use VSEPR, we first need to find the steric number (SN) of the central atom:

SN = (Number of atoms bonded to central atom) + (Number of lone pairs on central atom)

The steric number tells us the basic arrangement of the electron pairs (the "electron geometry"). The final shape of the molecule (the "molecular geometry") also depends on how many of those pairs are bonding pairs vs. lone pairs.

Phase 2: How to Get a Linear Shape

A linear molecule is one where all the atoms lie in a straight line, with a bond angle of 180°.

There are two common ways to achieve a linear molecular geometry based on the steric number:

  • SN = 2: Two bonding pairs, zero lone pairs (e.g., BeCl₂, CO₂). The two electron pairs get as far apart as possible, resulting in a 180° angle.
  • SN = 5: The electron geometry is trigonal bipyramidal. If there are 2 bonding pairs and 3 lone pairs, the lone pairs will occupy the three equatorial positions (a triangle in the middle) to minimize repulsion, forcing the two bonded atoms into the axial positions (top and bottom), resulting in a linear shape (e.g., XeF₂, I₃⁻).
