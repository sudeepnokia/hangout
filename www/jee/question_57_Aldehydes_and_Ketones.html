Learning Guide: Identifying Aldehydes with Fehling's Test

This guide explains a classic chemical test used to distinguish between aldehydes and ketones, and explores which types of compounds give a positive result.

Phase 1: Aldehydes vs. Ketones

Aldehydes and ketones both contain a carbonyl group, which is a carbon atom double-bonded to an oxygen atom (C=O).

  • In an aldehyde, the carbonyl carbon is bonded to at least one hydrogen atom. It's found at the end of a carbon chain. (General structure: R-CHO)
  • In a ketone, the carbonyl carbon is bonded to two other carbon atoms. It's found in the middle of a carbon chain. (General structure: R-CO-R')

This structural difference is key. The hydrogen atom attached to the carbonyl group in an aldehyde makes it easy to oxidize. Ketones, lacking this hydrogen, are much more resistant to oxidation.

Phase 2: The Test for Easy Oxidation

Fehling's test is designed to identify compounds that are easily oxidized. It uses a mild oxidizing agent, which is a solution containing Copper(II) ions, Cu²⁺, complexed with tartrate ions. This solution is deep blue.

The Reaction:

If an aldehyde is present, it gets oxidized to a carboxylate anion. In the process, the blue Cu²⁺ ions are reduced to Copper(I) ions, Cu⁺, which then precipitate out as red copper(I) oxide (Cu₂O).

Positive Test: Formation of a red precipitate. This indicates the presence of an aldehyde.

Negative Test: The solution remains blue. This indicates the absence of an aldehyde (i.e., it's a ketone or another non-oxidizable compound).

R-CHO (aldehyde) + 2Cu²⁺ (blue) + 5OH⁻ → R-COO⁻ (carboxylate) + Cu₂O(↓) (red) + 3H₂O

Generally, ketones do not give a positive Fehling's test.

Phase 3: The Exception - α-Hydroxy Ketones

There is a very important exception to the rule that ketones don't react.

An α-hydroxy ketone is a ketone that has a hydroxyl (-OH) group on the carbon atom immediately adjacent (the "alpha" carbon) to the carbonyl group.

Example: R-CO-CH(OH)-R'

Under the basic conditions of the Fehling's test, α-hydroxy ketones can undergo a rearrangement (called a tautomerization) to form an α-hydroxy aldehyde. This aldehyde can then be oxidized, giving a positive Fehling's test.

Carbohydrates like fructose are a famous example of α-hydroxy ketones that give a positive test.
