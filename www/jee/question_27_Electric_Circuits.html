Phase 1: Resistors in Parallel

When two resistors, r₁ and r₂, are connected in parallel, the equivalent resistance, r_eq, is given by the formula: 1/r_eq = 1/r₁ + 1/r₂.

This can be rearranged to r_eq = (r₁ * r₂) / (r₁ + r₂).

Question 1.1: If r₁ = 2Ω and r₂ = 3Ω, what is r_eq? Is r_eq smaller than both r₁ and r₂?
Show Solution

r_eq = (2 * 3) / (2 + 3) = 6 / 5 = 1.2Ω.

Yes, 1.2Ω is smaller than both 2Ω and 3Ω. This is always true for resistors in parallel. The equivalent resistance is always smaller than the smallest individual resistance.

This directly proves that Statement-II of the problem is true.

Phase 2: EMF and Internal Resistance

An ideal battery provides a constant voltage, called its EMF (ε). A real-world ("nonideal") battery has this EMF but also a small internal resistance (r). This is like having a tiny resistor inside the battery itself.

When we connect two nonideal batteries in parallel, we need to find both the equivalent EMF (ε_eq) and the equivalent internal resistance (r_eq).

Phase 3: Equivalent EMF for Parallel Batteries

The formula for the equivalent EMF of two batteries in parallel is a weighted average:

ε_eq = (ε₁/r₁ + ε₂/r₂) / (1/r₁ + 1/r₂) = (ε₁r₂ + ε₂r₁) / (r₁ + r₂)

Question 3.1: Let's say we have a 10V battery (ε₁) with 2Ω internal resistance (r₁) and a 4V battery (ε₂) with 1Ω internal resistance (r₂). What is the equivalent EMF?
Show Solution

ε_eq = (10*1 + 4*2) / (2 + 1) = (10 + 8) / 3 = 18 / 3 = 6V.

Notice that the result, 6V, is between the two individual EMFs (4V and 10V). It is smaller than the larger EMF (10V) but larger than the smaller EMF (4V).

This means the statement "The equivalent emf... is smaller than either of the two emfs" is not always true. It is only smaller than the larger of the two.

Therefore, Statement-I is false.
