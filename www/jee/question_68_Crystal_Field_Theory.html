Learning Guide: Crystal Field Splitting Energy (CFSE)

This guide explains how ligands affect the d-orbitals of a central metal ion and how to determine the Crystal Field Splitting Energy (CFSE).

Phase 1: d-Orbital Splitting

In a free metal ion, the five d-orbitals (dxy, dyz, dxz, dx²-y², dz²) are all at the same energy level (they are "degenerate").

When ligands approach the metal ion to form an octahedral complex, they repel the electrons in the d-orbitals. This repulsion is not uniform.

  • The dx²-y² and dz² orbitals point directly at the incoming ligands, so they experience strong repulsion and their energy level increases significantly. This higher energy set is called the e_g set.
  • The dxy, dyz, and dxz orbitals point between the ligands, so they experience less repulsion and their energy level decreases. This lower energy set is called the t₂g set.

The energy difference between the t₂g and e_g sets is called the Crystal Field Splitting Energy (Δ₀).

Phase 2: Filling the Orbitals

How electrons fill these split orbitals depends on the strength of the ligands.

Strong-field ligands (like CN⁻, en, NH₃) cause a large split (Δ₀). It takes a lot of energy for an electron to jump up to the e_g level. So, electrons will pair up in the lower t₂g orbitals first before occupying the e_g orbitals. This is called a low-spin complex.

Weak-field ligands (like SCN⁻, Cl⁻, F⁻) cause a small split (Δ₀). It's easier for an electron to jump up to the e_g level than to pair up in a t₂g orbital (which costs pairing energy). So, electrons will occupy all five orbitals singly before any pairing occurs. This is called a high-spin complex.

Phase 3: The CFSE Formula

The t₂g orbitals are stabilized (energy goes down) by -0.4 Δ₀ each, and the e_g orbitals are destabilized (energy goes up) by +0.6 Δ₀ each.

CFSE Formula: CFSE = (-0.4 * n_t2g + 0.6 * n_eg) * Δ₀

Where n_t2g is the number of electrons in the t₂g orbitals and n_eg is the number of electrons in the e_g orbitals.

Question: When would the CFSE be equal to zero?

The CFSE would be zero if the stabilization from the t₂g electrons perfectly cancels out the destabilization from the e_g electrons.

-0.4 * n_t2g + 0.6 * n_eg = 0

This can happen in two specific, highly symmetrical electron configurations:

  1. d⁰ configuration: No d-electrons, so n_t2g=0 and n_eg=0. CFSE = 0.
  2. d¹⁰ configuration: All d-orbitals are full. n_t2g=6 and n_eg=4.
    CFSE = (-0.4 * 6 + 0.6 * 4)Δ₀ = (-2.4 + 2.4)Δ₀ = 0.
  3. d⁵ high-spin configuration: The ligands are weak, so electrons fill all orbitals singly. n_t2g=3 and n_eg=2.
    CFSE = (-0.4 * 3 + 0.6 * 2)Δ₀ = (-1.2 + 1.2)Δ₀ = 0.

Phase 4: The Summit - The JEE Main Question

Original Question: In which of the following complexes the CFSE, Δ₀ will be equal to zero?

  1. [Fe(en)₃]Cl₃
  2. K₄[Fe(CN)₆]
  3. [Fe(NH₃)₆]Br₂
  4. K₃[Fe(SCN)₆]

We need to find the complex that has a d⁰, d¹⁰, or d⁵ high-spin configuration for the central iron (Fe) ion.

1. [Fe(en)₃]Cl₃:

  • The overall charge is 0. Cl has a -1 charge, so 3 Cl⁻ means the complex ion is [Fe(en)₃]³⁺.
  • Ethylenediamine (en) is a neutral ligand. So, the oxidation state of Fe is +3.
  • Fe (Z=26) is [Ar] 3d⁶4s². Fe³⁺ is [Ar] 3d⁵. This is a d⁵ configuration.
  • 'en' is a strong-field ligand. This will force pairing, creating a low-spin complex (t₂g⁵ e_g⁰). CFSE will not be zero.

2. K₄[Fe(CN)₆]:

  • 4 K⁺ means the complex ion is [Fe(CN)₆]⁴⁻.
  • Cyanide (CN⁻) has a -1 charge. Let Fe be x. x + 6(-1) = -4 → x = +2.
  • Fe²⁺ is [Ar] 3d⁶. This is a d⁶ configuration. CFSE cannot be zero.

3. [Fe(NH₃)₆]Br₂:

  • 2 Br⁻ means the complex ion is [Fe(NH₃)₆]²⁺.
  • Ammonia (NH₃) is neutral. So, the oxidation state of Fe is +2.
  • Fe²⁺ is [Ar] 3d⁶. This is a d⁶ configuration. CFSE cannot be zero.

4. K₃[Fe(SCN)₆]:

  • 3 K⁺ means the complex ion is [Fe(SCN)₆]³⁻.
  • Thiocyanate (SCN⁻) has a -1 charge. Let Fe be x. x + 6(-1) = -3 → x = +3.
  • Fe³⁺ is [Ar] 3d⁵. This is a d⁵ configuration.
  • SCN⁻ is a weak-field ligand. This will result in a high-spin complex.
  • In a d⁵ high-spin complex, electrons fill as t₂g³ e_g².
  • Let's calculate the CFSE: CFSE = (-0.4 * 3 + 0.6 * 2)Δ₀ = (-1.2 + 1.2)Δ₀ = 0.

This complex has zero CFSE.
