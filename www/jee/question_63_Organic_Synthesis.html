Learning Guide: A Multi-Step Aromatic Synthesis

This guide breaks down a four-step organic synthesis, explaining the purpose and outcome of each reaction.

Phase 1: The Reagent Toolkit

Let's understand what each set of reagents does. The starting material is nitrobenzene.

(i) Br₂, AcOH (Acetic Acid): This is an electrophilic aromatic substitution, specifically bromination. The nitro group (-NO₂) is a strong deactivator and a meta-director. This means it makes the benzene ring less reactive and directs any new substituent to the meta position (carbon 3 or 5).

(ii) Sn, HCl: This is a standard reagent for the reduction of a nitro group (-NO₂) to an amino group (-NH₂). The tin metal in acidic solution is a powerful reducing agent for this specific transformation.

(iii) NaNO₂, HCl, 273 K (0°C): This is the diazotization reaction. It converts a primary aromatic amine (-NH₂) into a diazonium salt (-N₂⁺Cl⁻). This reaction must be kept cold because diazonium salts are unstable at higher temperatures.

(iv) C₂H₅OH (Ethanol): Ethanol can act as a reducing agent for diazonium salts, replacing the entire -N₂⁺ group with a hydrogen atom (-H). This is a common deamination reaction.

Let's trace the starting material through each step.

Starting Material: Nitrobenzene

Step (i): Bromination (Br₂, AcOH)

The -NO₂ group directs the incoming Br to the meta position.

Product of Step (i): m-Bromonitrobenzene

Step (ii): Reduction (Sn, HCl)

The nitro group (-NO₂) is reduced to an amino group (-NH₂). The bromine is unaffected.

Product of Step (ii): m-Bromoaniline. Let's call this compound A.

Step (iii): Diazotization (NaNO₂, HCl, 0°C)

The amino group (-NH₂) is converted to a diazonium salt group (-N₂⁺Cl⁻).

Product of Step (iii): m-Bromobenzenediazonium chloride.

Step (iv): Reaction with Ethanol (C₂H₅OH)

This step involves two simultaneous reactions. The diazonium salt is the main reactant.

  1. Deamination: The primary reaction is the reduction of the diazonium salt. The entire -N₂⁺ group is replaced by a hydrogen atom. This converts our molecule back to bromobenzene.
  2. Ether Formation: Ethanol (C₂H₅OH) can also act as a nucleophile and attack the diazonium salt, but more importantly, it can react with the diazonium salt in a substitution reaction to form an ether. The ethoxy group (-OC₂H₅) replaces the diazonium group. This gives m-Bromoethoxybenzene.
  3. Oxidation of Ethanol: In the process of reducing the diazonium salt, the ethanol itself gets oxidized. Ethanol (CH₃CH₂OH) is oxidized to ethanal (acetaldehyde, CH₃CHO).
