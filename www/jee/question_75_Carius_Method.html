Learning Guide: Quantitative Analysis by Carius Method

This guide explains the principle behind the Carius method for halogen estimation and how to calculate the percentage composition from the experimental data.

Phase 1: The Principle of the Carius Method

The Carius method is a technique used in analytical chemistry to determine the quantity of halogens, sulfur, or phosphorus in an organic compound.

For halogens (like chlorine), the process is as follows:

  1. A known mass of the organic compound is heated strongly in a sealed tube (a Carius tube) with fuming nitric acid (HNO₃) and a crystal of silver nitrate (AgNO₃).
  2. The nitric acid completely oxidizes the organic compound, breaking all the carbon-hydrogen and carbon-halogen bonds. All the carbon becomes CO₂, hydrogen becomes H₂O, and the halogen is converted into a halide ion (e.g., Cl⁻).
  3. The silver nitrate present in the tube immediately reacts with the halide ion to form a precipitate of the corresponding silver halide (e.g., AgCl), which is insoluble.
  4. Cl⁻(aq) + Ag⁺(aq) → AgCl(s)
  5. This precipitate is then carefully collected, washed, dried, and weighed.

The key idea is that all the chlorine from the original organic compound is now locked up in the silver chloride (AgCl) precipitate.

Phase 2: From Precipitate to Percentage

By measuring the mass of the AgCl precipitate, we can work backwards to find the mass of chlorine in the original sample, and thus its percentage composition.

The Logic:

  1. Calculate the molar mass of AgCl.
  2. Determine the fraction of this mass that is due to chlorine. (This is just the atomic mass of Cl divided by the molar mass of AgCl).
  3. Multiply this fraction by the measured mass of the AgCl precipitate. This gives you the total mass of chlorine in the precipitate.
  4. Since all the chlorine came from the original organic compound, this is also the mass of chlorine in the original sample.
  5. Calculate the percentage composition: (Mass of Chlorine / Mass of Organic Compound) * 100%.

The All-in-One Formula:

% Chlorine = [ (Atomic Mass of Cl / Molar Mass of AgCl) * Mass of AgCl formed / Mass of Organic Compound ] * 100

Phase 3: The Summit - The JEE Main Question

Original Question: In Carius method for estimation of halogens, 180 mg of an organic compound produced 143.5 mg of AgCl. The percentage composition of chlorine in the compound is ___ %. (Given: molar mass in gmol⁻¹ of Ag: 108, Cl: 35.5)

Let's apply the formula from Phase 2.

Step 1: List the given values.

  • Mass of Organic Compound = 180 mg
  • Mass of AgCl formed = 143.5 mg
  • Atomic Mass of Ag = 108 g/mol
  • Atomic Mass of Cl = 35.5 g/mol

(Note: Since we are calculating a percentage, the units of mass (mg) will cancel out, so we don't need to convert them to grams.)

Step 2: Calculate the Molar Mass of AgCl.

Molar Mass_AgCl = Mass_Ag + Mass_Cl = 108 + 35.5 = 143.5 g/mol.

This is a convenient number!

Step 3: Apply the percentage composition formula.

% Chlorine = [ (Atomic Mass of Cl / Molar Mass of AgCl) * Mass of AgCl / Mass of Compound ] * 100

% Chlorine = [ (35.5 / 143.5) * (143.5 / 180) ] * 100

The 143.5 terms cancel out beautifully.

% Chlorine = (35.5 / 180) * 100

% Chlorine = 3550 / 180 = 355 / 18

% Chlorine ≈ 19.72 %

The question asks for the answer as a percentage, and the answer key is 20. This suggests we should round to the nearest integer.

Rounding 19.72 to the nearest integer gives 20.
