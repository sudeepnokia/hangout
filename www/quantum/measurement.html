The Measurement Postulate

The power of a quantum computer lies in its ability to exist in a superposition of many states. However, to get a result from a quantum computation, we must measure the qubits. The act of measurement is a crucial, and often misunderstood, part of quantum mechanics.

When we measure a qubit, its superposition collapses. The qubit is forced to choose one of its possible classical states (0 or 1). This process is probabilistic. The probability of measuring a particular state is determined by the amplitude of that state in the superposition. For a qubit in the state `α|0⟩ + β|1⟩`, the probability of measuring `|0⟩` is `|α|²`, and the probability of measuring `|1⟩` is `|β|²`.

The Observer Effect

A key consequence of the measurement postulate is that you cannot measure a quantum state without disturbing it. The very act of observing the system changes it. This is a fundamental departure from classical physics, where we can (in principle) observe a system without affecting it.

This means we cannot directly see the superposition. We can only see the classical outcome after the superposition has collapsed. Quantum algorithms are designed to cleverly manipulate the probabilities so that when we do measure, the state we want is the one that is most likely to appear.
