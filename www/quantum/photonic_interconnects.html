The Modularity Advantage

Building a single, monolithic quantum computer with millions of high-quality qubits is incredibly difficult. A more promising approach is to build smaller, more manageable quantum modules and then connect them together to form a larger, more powerful machine. This is the idea behind modular quantum computing.

Why Photonics?

Connecting these modules is a major challenge. Superconducting wires, which are used inside the modules, are not suitable for long-distance communication. This is where photonics comes in. Photons (particles of light) are excellent carriers of information. They can travel long distances with very little loss, and they are immune to the kind of electrical noise that can plague conventional wires.

The Transduction Challenge

The biggest hurdle in photonic interconnects is quantum transduction: the process of converting quantum information from one physical form to another. In this case, we need to convert the information from a superconducting qubit (which operates at microwave frequencies) to a photon (which operates at optical frequencies), and then back again, all without destroying the delicate quantum state.

Approaches to Transduction

This is an active area of research, with several promising approaches:

  • Opto-mechanical systems: Using tiny mechanical resonators to bridge the gap between microwave and optical frequencies.
  • Electro-optic modulators: Using special materials that can change their optical properties in response to an electric field.
  • Rare-earth ions: Using certain types of atoms that can interact with both microwaves and light.
