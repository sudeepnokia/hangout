The Challenge of Building a Qubit

A qubit is not a naturally occurring object. It is a carefully engineered quantum system. The primary challenge in qubit fabrication is to create a system that can be reliably controlled, is well-isolated from environmental noise (decoherence), and can be manufactured at scale with high uniformity.

Types of Qubits

There are many different physical systems being explored as potential qubits. Some of the leading candidates include:

  • Superconducting Qubits: These are currently one of the most popular approaches. They are based on tiny superconducting circuits with Josephson junctions. They are relatively large (microns to millimeters) and can be fabricated using standard lithography techniques.
  • Trapped Ion Qubits: These use individual ions (charged atoms) held in place by electromagnetic fields. They have very long coherence times but can be difficult to scale.
  • Photonic Qubits: These use single photons as qubits. They are excellent for communication but it is challenging to make them interact for computation.
  • Silicon Spin Qubits: These leverage the spin of an electron trapped in a silicon-based structure, often called a "quantum dot." A major advantage is that they can potentially be manufactured using existing CMOS fabrication infrastructure.

Leveraging VLSI for Quantum

The incredible success of classical computing is due in large part to the power of Very Large Scale Integration (VLSI). The goal is to apply the same principles to quantum computing.

From Lab to Fab

Early quantum devices were built manually in research labs. To build a useful quantum computer with millions of qubits, we need to move to an industrial fabrication model (a "fab"). This means using the same automated processes that are used to create the chips in our phones and computers.

The Promise of Silicon

This is where silicon spin qubits become very attractive. The global semiconductor industry has spent decades perfecting the art of silicon manufacturing. If we can build high-quality qubits in silicon, we can tap into this massive existing infrastructure. This is a key focus of the "Quantum VLSI" field.
