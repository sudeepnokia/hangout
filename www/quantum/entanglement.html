A Mysterious Connection

Quantum entanglement is one of the most counter-intuitive, yet powerful, phenomena in quantum mechanics. It occurs when two or more quantum particles become linked in such a way that their fates are intertwined, regardless of the distance separating them. Measuring a property of one particle instantaneously influences the corresponding property of the other particle(s).

Analogy: Imagine you have a pair of gloves, one left and one right. You put them in separate, identical boxes and mail one to a friend on the other side of the world. Before anyone opens a box, you don't know which box contains which glove. But the moment your friend opens their box and finds a left glove, you instantly know that your box contains the right glove. Entanglement is like this, but with a quantum twist: before measurement, the particles are not in a definite state (like the gloves being pre-determined). They are in a superposition of all possibilities, and it is the act of measurement on one that forces the other into a definite state.

Creating and Using Entanglement

Entanglement is a resource that can be used to perform powerful computations. It is created by applying specific quantum gates (like the CNOT gate) to a set of qubits. Once entangled, these qubits can be used for tasks like quantum teleportation and superdense coding, and they are a key ingredient in many quantum algorithms, such as Shor's algorithm for factoring.

The correlations between entangled particles are stronger than any correlations possible in classical physics. This has been experimentally verified many times, proving that entanglement is a real and fundamental aspect of our universe.
