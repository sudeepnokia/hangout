Manipulating Qubits

Just as classical computers use logic gates (like AND, OR, and NOT) to process information, quantum computers use quantum gates to manipulate the state of qubits. A quantum gate is a basic quantum circuit operating on a small number of qubits. They are the building blocks of quantum algorithms.

Quantum gates are represented by unitary matrices. When a quantum gate is applied to a qubit, the matrix representing the gate is multiplied by the vector representing the qubit's state. This corresponds to a rotation of the qubit's state vector on the Bloch sphere.

Common Quantum Gates

Single-Qubit Gates

  • Hadamard Gate (H): This is one of the most important gates. It takes a qubit from a classical state (0 or 1) and puts it into a perfect superposition of both.
  • Pauli-X Gate (X): The quantum equivalent of a classical NOT gate. It flips the state of a qubit from `|0⟩` to `|1⟩` and vice versa.
  • Pauli-Y Gate (Y): A rotation around the Y-axis of the Bloch sphere.
  • Pauli-Z Gate (Z): A rotation around the Z-axis of the Bloch sphere. It introduces a phase shift.

Two-Qubit Gates

Two-qubit gates are essential for creating entanglement between qubits, which is necessary for most powerful quantum algorithms.

  • Controlled-NOT Gate (CNOT): This gate has two input qubits: a control qubit and a target qubit. It flips the target qubit if and only if the control qubit is in the state `|1⟩`.
  • SWAP Gate: This gate swaps the states of two qubits.
