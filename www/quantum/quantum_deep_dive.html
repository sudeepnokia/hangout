The Mathematics of Qubits

While the spinning coin analogy is useful, the true language of quantum mechanics is mathematics. A qubit's state is represented by a vector in a 2D complex vector space.

Bra-ket Notation

We use Dirac's bra-ket notation to describe quantum states. A "ket" `|ψ⟩` represents a column vector, and a "bra" `⟨ψ|` represents a row vector (specifically, the conjugate transpose). The classical states 0 and 1 are represented by basis vectors:
`|0⟩ = [1, 0]`
`|1⟩ = [0, 1]`
A general qubit state `|ψ⟩` in superposition is a linear combination: `|ψ⟩ = α|0⟩ + β|1⟩`, where `α` and `β` are complex numbers called probability amplitudes. The constraint is that `|α|² + |β|² = 1`, which means the probabilities of measuring 0 or 1 must sum to 100%.

The Bloch Sphere

The Bloch sphere is a geometrical representation of the state space of a single qubit. The north pole typically represents `|0⟩`, the south pole represents `|1⟩`, and any other point on the surface of the sphere represents a unique superposition state. Quantum gates can be visualized as rotations of the state vector on the Bloch sphere.

Quantum Gates and Algorithms

Quantum algorithms are sequences of quantum gates that manipulate qubit states to solve problems.

Single-Qubit Gates

These gates operate on a single qubit. Examples include:

  • Hadamard Gate (H): Creates a perfect superposition. `H|0⟩ = (|0⟩ + |1⟩)/√2`.
  • Pauli-X Gate (X): The quantum equivalent of a NOT gate. It flips `|0⟩` to `|1⟩` and vice-versa.
  • Pauli-Z Gate (Z): Flips the phase of the `|1⟩` state.

Multi-Qubit Gates

These gates operate on two or more qubits and are essential for creating entanglement.

  • Controlled-NOT Gate (CNOT): A two-qubit gate that flips the second qubit (the target) if and only if the first qubit (the control) is in the state `|1⟩`. This is a fundamental gate for creating entanglement.

Famous Quantum Algorithms

  • Shor's Algorithm: Provides an exponential speedup for factoring large numbers, posing a threat to classical cryptography.
  • Grover's Algorithm: Offers a quadratic speedup for searching unstructured databases.
